Work

This will be mostly giving a short summary of some of my publications and some conference papers I'm writing. I have a lot of other work in the pipeline to be published soon. This will update as they get published.

  • My latest publication (Ghouse et al 2021, Physical Review E) is about using Gaussian processes to infer directionality of coupled physical systems using the principles of convergent cross-mapping (CCM--Sugihara et al 2012, Science). The main idea, as illustrated in (Sugihara et al 2012, Science) is that delay-coordinate maps on a single variable time series are diffeomorphic (gradient preserving) to the original system. This should imply that if we reconstruct phase spaces using delay-coordinates from two different time series, we should see information of putative (causal) variables retrievable in the phase space reconstructed from the caused variable. On the other hand, all information of the caused variable may not be able to be retrieved from a phase space reconstructed from a time series reconstructed from the putative variable.

    My contribution is to place a probability distribution on each point in the phase space. Doing that, we can construct likelihood ratio tests, similar to what you may see in a discriminant analysis test, to infer the most likely direction of coupling between these two a priori assumed coupled variables we observe through some measured time series. More concretely, we assume the phase space reconstructed from a time series is a realization of a Gaussian process with an a priori mean and covariance function. Determining point estimates of parameters of the a prior functions from a maximum marginal likelihood optimization, we can then determine the a posteriori likelihood of a phase space reconstructed from a variable we're testing to see is causing the variable that optimized the gaussian process mean and covariance functions.

  • My conference submission to the Organization of Human Brain mapping concerns using multivoxel pattern analysis on cortical surface reconstructions to decode neural representations of tasting when there is a nutrition claim. Food claims in general have a large literature of research, most famously the Coke vs. Pepsi paper (McClure et al 2004, Neuron), where it was found that preferences can be decoded particularly in the ventromedial prefrontal cortex. Furthermore, the neural food valuation is modified by nutrition claims, i.e. what is told to the consumer about the food (Enax and Weber 2017, JAFIO).

    My main curiosity, personally, is how the active sensory experience is modulated by the fact that there's a nutrition claim. To be honest, the fact that the general sensory experience can be impacted by the stories we tell ourselves, or the stories we hear is curious. Particularly, it's fascinating to think that there's an intrinsic coupling between what our expectations of a sensation may be and the sensation itself. It kind of gives some credence to the mind over matter mantra. Though, of course I'd not claim that at an absolute.

    Another thing that was fun was learning about very advanced methodology used in functional magnetic resonance imaging (fMRI) analysis. For example, the decoding analysis pipeline, constructing classifiers and exploring the size of radii and their effect at uncovering neural representations, i.e. the spatial scale of local neural codes, was a fun foray of research. Furthermore, understanding the process of cortical surface reconstruction was pretty fun too. How different matters were segmented and mapped to an inflated surface, and how to standardize results over varying anatomical surfaces, was cool.

  • My first "first author" publication (Fischer, Ghouse et al 2020, Journal of Neurotrauma) was effectively my master's thesis work to develop a machine learning model that uses the methodology we used in our laboratory back in Barcelona--i.e. diffuse correlation spectroscopy (DCS)--to predict intracranial pressure. The skull can be seen as an incompressible box that can only be consumed by brain matter, blood, and cerebral spinal fluid. Brain is a constant, and as intracranial pressure changes, that means either one of blood of cerebral spinal fluid increased (Monroe-Kelly doctrine). This is especially important to monitor during a brain injury, or for hydrocephalus, where swelling may occur due to an increase of cerebral spinal fluid for example. This could impair perfusion of blood oxygen to tissue, given by the important formula CPP = MAP- ICP, where the perfusion pressure (CPP) would decrease as intracranial pressure (ICP) increases and mean arterial pressure (MAP) stays the same. My solution was effectively to make a recurrent neural network that takes standardized blood flow time series observed by DCS in the frontal regions of the head to analyze the time dynamics of the signals in order to see if it can be mapped to invasive intracranial pressures. Though the original goal was to classify severity levels of ICP (low, medium, high), we found that we were able to determine absolute pressure, which was fascinating. Even if absolute pressure were unreliable, at least being able to determine dynamical features like intracranial pressure waves can provide the neurotrauma doctors important information on the health of the patient!

  • My first paper I published in my PhD (Ghouse et al 2020, Entropy) was an analysis of various entropy analyses in reconstructed phase spaces to see if it added any additional information in nonparametric statistical rank analysis for uncovering neural correlates from fNIRS. To be honest, it was a very introductory work to get me up to speed with the foundations of my PhD work.

About me

"I want inside this night that is longer than life. I want, inside this night, life raw and bloody and full of saliva. I want this word: splendidness, splendidness is the fruit in its succulence, fruit without sadness. I want distances. My wild intuition about myself. But my main thing is always hidden. I am implicit. And when I make myself explicit I lose the humid intimacy." -- Clarice Lispector, Agua Viva

I've been working in computational neuroscience the past 3 years of my PhD under the guise of a program of information engineering at the University of Pisa. I enjoy learning about neural mechanisms, representations of cognitive states in the brain, emergent phenomenon that come from dynamic and complex systems, and maths and physics in general.

I think my passion for cognitive science came from a cathartic moment in my early 20s when I realized one--just how little I knew about myself, why I feel things, why I succumb to certain patterns and two--my general limited understanding of the complexity of the conscious experience of others. I first delved into big literature to flourish and tickle this intense curiosity of mine. Then, considering I was near the end of my bachelor's program in biomedical engineering, I tried to reconcile that career path I was on with this uncovered desire of knowledge I had. I have been really fortunate to have such inspiring interactions during whlie living in Barcelona for three years, meeting some of the most brilliant people I know at Institut de Ciencies Fotonicas--where I further fostered deep fondness for physics and maths--and Universitat Pompeu Fabra--at my masters where I could really focus more on intimate matters in the philosophy of cognitive sciences and bleeding edge research in cognition, and its computational aspects, etc. All this built me up to work on the edge of cognitive sciences with computational neurosciences, particularly the concept of dynamical and complex systems, umbrella terms for which the brain falls under. Some of my work does also try to reconcile perceptions, awareness, decision making, reward circuitry, motor cognition, etc.

I wish to find more experience, whether that's in industry or academia. I truly believe greater knowledge of abstract neural mechanics, dynamics, large scale interactions, embodied conscious experience, etc, can be a powerful frontier to cross. I believe this is true both for our general understanding of our own conscious experience, and two for studying many complex psychological phenomena or aiding in psychological impairments

When I'm not doing that, you can find me writing poetry, playing my guitar or learning a new instrument, learning and creating audiovisual arts, or reading books.
